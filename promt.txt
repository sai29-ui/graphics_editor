Those lines form a coordinate ruler (X-axis ruler) printed at the bottom of the canvas in CLI mode. They help you find the exact column index (X-coordinate) on the text grid when you want to add or verify shape positions.

Here is how to read them:

1. The Canvas Border
+------------------------------------------------------------+ This is the bottom border of your 60-character-wide canvas.

2. The Tens Row (Ruler Line 1)
 0    +    1    +    2    +    3    +    4    +    5    +

The numbers 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 represent the tens digit of the column (0,10,20,30,40,50).
The + symbols mark the mid-points (5,15,25,35,45,55).
3. The Units Row (Ruler Line 2)
012345678901234567890123456789012345678901234567890123456789

This row prints the units digit of the column index (0 through 9 repeating).
Example (How to use it):
If you want to find the coordinate for column 24:

Look at the tens row to locate the column block starting with 2 (which represents column 20).
Move 4 characters to the right using the units row below it to land on 4.
That column on the canvas corresponds exactly to X=24.

whats the use of 9. Toggle Circle Aspect Ratio Correction (ON)
10. Set Background Character (Current: ' ')
5:38 PM, 6/7/2026


Here is what those two options do:

9. Toggle Circle Aspect Ratio Correction (ON/OFF)
The Problem: In terminals, text character cells are rectangular rather than square. Typically, a character is about twice as tall as it is wide (a 2:1 aspect ratio). If you draw a mathematically perfect circle (equal radius in columns and rows), it will look like a vertical oval (stretched vertically) on your screen.
What it does:
When ON (default): The program automatically doubles the horizontal offset (X-direction) of the circle's points. This compensates for the rectangular terminal characters and makes the circle look round and circular on your screen.
When OFF: The circle is plotted raw on the coordinate grid without correction. It will look like a vertically stretched oval on screen, but it will be mathematically symmetric in terms of grid indices.
10. Set Background Character (Current: ' ')
What it does: This changes the character used to fill the empty space of your canvas (which defaults to space ' ').
Why it is useful:
If you draw shapes using _ (underscore) against a blank space background, it can be hard to see the canvas boundaries or separate shapes.
By setting the background character to something like a dot (.), a dash (-), or a tilde (~), the entire empty area of the canvas is filled with that pattern. Your drawn shapes (using * or _) will then stand out clearly against the background.
